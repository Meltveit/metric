About Weight Units

Weight, more accurately called mass, is a measure of the amount of matter in an object. In the International System of Units (SI), the basic unit of mass is the kilogram (kg).

Different weight units are used in different regions of the world. The metric system (kilograms, grams) is used in most countries, while the imperial system (pounds, ounces) is commonly used in the United States and some other countries.

Unit Definitions

  • Kilogram (kg): The SI base unit of mass, defined by the International Prototype of the Kilogram until 2019, now defined using the Planck constant.
  • Gram (g): Equal to 1/1000 of a kilogram.
  • Pound (lb): Equal to 0.45359237 kilograms in the modern definition.
  • Ounce (oz): Equal to 1/16 of a pound, or about 28.35 grams.
  • Stone (st): Equal to 14 pounds, or about 6.35 kilograms. Commonly used in the UK and Ireland.
  • Metric Ton (t): Equal to 1,000 kilograms.
  • Carat (ct): Equal to 0.2 grams, used for measuring gemstones and pearls.
