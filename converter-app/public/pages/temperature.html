About Temperature Units

Temperature is a physical quantity that expresses hot and cold. It is fundamentally linked to the kinetic energy of the particles which make up matter.

Different temperature scales are used in different regions of the world. The Celsius scale is used in most countries, while the Fahrenheit scale is primarily used in the United States. The Kelvin scale is the SI base unit and is used in scientific contexts.

Temperature Scales

  • Celsius (°C): A scale where 0°C is the freezing point of water and 100°C is the boiling point of water at standard atmospheric pressure.
  • Fahrenheit (°F): A scale where 32°F is the freezing point of water and 212°F is the boiling point of water at standard atmospheric pressure.
  • Kelvin (K): The SI base unit of temperature. 0 K is absolute zero, the theoretical absence of all thermal energy. There are no negative temperatures in the Kelvin scale.
  • Rankine (°R): An absolute temperature scale using Fahrenheit degrees, where 0°R is absolute zero.
  • Réaumur (°Ré): A temperature scale where 0°Ré is the freezing point of water and 80°Ré is the boiling point of water.

Conversion Formulas

  • Celsius to Fahrenheit: °F = (°C × 9/5) + 32
  • Fahrenheit to Celsius: °C = (°F - 32) × 5/9
  • Celsius to Kelvin: K = °C + 273.15
  • Kelvin to Celsius: °C = K - 273.15
  • Fahrenheit to Kelvin: K = (°F - 32) × 5/9 + 273.15
  • Kelvin to Fahrenheit: °F = (K - 273.15) × 9/5 + 32
